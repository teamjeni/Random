Hello, I am a geologist that has had the opportunity to stumble upon some interesting and beautiful gemstone deposits during my field work. I have been interested in gems and minerals as far back as I can remember. When I was a student, my wife and I had the chance to take a silversmith class, after which we bought a combination lapidary unit to cut stones with -- which I did with relish. As life went on, more and more time was spent on career matters, silversmith work and lapidary fell by the wayside.
 

This all changed about nine years ago while doing a project in Fish Lake Valley, Nevada. The other geologist working with me had discovered a turquoise deposit near Gabbs, Nevada in the 1970s while working on a molybdenum porphyry exploration project for Bear Creek Mining – the Kennecott Copper exploration arm at the time. Since the area was already under claim for molybdenum at the time, all he could do was walk away. This discovery soon leaked out and unknown persons mined the deposit as the Gabbs Turquoise mine. They abandoned the deposit, after a period of open pit mining, likely thinking that it was exhausted.

 
In 2004 my geology colleague and great friend suggested that we return to this discovery and see if anything was left. It was apparent that the original miners had missed a fair bit of turquoise, so we filed a claim on it and called it the Paradise #1. Since this time we have mined the spoils left behind by the previous occupants and do a little hard rock mining when we feel energetic. Our efforts have led to the partial reclamation of the original pit and we have found some excellent turquoise.


The following year, while mapping geology in the Fish Lake Valley area, we stumbled on to another old mine that, much to our surprise, contained turquoise and variscite. We claimed this deposit as the Silver Peak #1. A couple of weeks later we discovered another close by variscite deposit, claimed it, and named it the Silver Peak #2. The stones in our Etsy store are currently all from these three mines. However, in 2012, while prospecting for kaolinite, my wife and I discovered an old hot springs opaline sinter in Millard County, Utah that had secondary veins of fire opal. This became the Rojo Vivo claim. We have not cut much of this material yet, but it to will appear in our Etsy store someday.


My wife has fallen in love with wire wrapping, so this artwork originates from her while I do the cabbing. We both share in silversmith work.

Thank you for taking the time to learn a little about us and our mines.
